Cardiac Conditions

Comprehensive clinical research from ACC/AHA, ESC, NICE and CSI guidelines.

⚠
All information is for educational research. Cardiac conditions require clinical examination, ECG, and appropriate investigations. Never delay assessment for chest pain or breathlessness.

Quick Clinical Reference

Chest Pain Red Flags

  • Central crushing chest pain radiating to arm/jaw/neck
  • Associated sweating, nausea, breathlessness = ACS until proven otherwise
  • Sudden tearing/ripping pain = consider aortic dissection
  • Atypical presentation common in women, elderly, diabetics

Heart Failure Four Pillars

  • ACE-I/ARB or ARNI (sacubitril-valsartan preferred)
  • Beta-blocker (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol succinate)
  • Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (spironolactone)
  • SGLT2 inhibitor (benefit regardless of diabetes status)

AF Stroke Risk (CHA2DS2-VASc)

  • Heart failure, Hypertension, Age≥75(2), Diabetes
  • Stroke/TIA history(2), Vascular disease, Age 65-74, female Sex
  • Score guides anticoagulation decision
  • DOACs preferred over warfarin for most patients

South Asian CVD Risk

  • CAD occurs 5-10 years earlier than Western populations
  • Higher cardiometabolic risk at lower BMI thresholds
  • Elevated Lp(a) common, low HDL/high triglyceride pattern
  • Risk calculators often underestimate South Asian risk
Leading Cause of Death

Coronary Artery Disease

Research on stable angina, acute coronary syndrome, and revascularisation from ACC/AHA and ESC guidelines.

Overview and Risk Factors

Atherosclerotic narrowing of coronary arteries. Leading cause of death globally. India: CAD occurs 5-10 years earlier and progresses more aggressively than in Western populations.

Risk Factors

  • Non-modifiable: age, male sex, family history of premature CAD
  • Modifiable: hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidaemia (high Lp(a) common in Indians), smoking, obesity
  • South Asian ethnicity itself is an independent risk factor requiring lower treatment thresholds

Stable Angina

Predictable chest pain on exertion, relieved by rest or GTN within minutes. Central/retrosternal, may radiate to arm/jaw/neck.

Investigation

CT coronary angiography (CTCA) first-line per NICE. Functional imaging if inconclusive. Invasive angiography if high-risk.

Management

Beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers first-line antianginal. Aspirin, statin, ACE inhibitor. Revascularisation (PCI/CABG) for refractory symptoms.

Acute Coronary Syndrome

Spectrum: unstable angina, NSTEMI, STEMI. STEMI is an EMERGENCY requiring immediate reperfusion.

STEMI

Primary PCI (door-to-balloon under 90 minutes) or thrombolysis if PCI unavailable within 120 minutes.

NSTEMI/Unstable Angina

GRACE score guides timing of invasive strategy. Dual antiplatelet therapy, anticoagulation, beta-blocker, high-intensity statin.

Post-MI

Cardiac rehabilitation, secondary prevention medications lifelong, address all modifiable risk factors.

Four Pillars Therapy

Heart Failure

Research on classification, aetiology, and evidence-based treatment from ESC and ACC/AHA guidelines.

Classification and Aetiology

HFrEF (EF≤40%), HFmrEF (EF 41-49%), HFpEF (EF≥50% — increasingly common, associated with hypertension/diabetes/obesity/AF). NYHA I-IV functional classification.

Causes

Ischaemic heart disease (most common globally), hypertensive heart disease, dilated cardiomyopathy, valvular disease (rheumatic heart disease significant in India, unlike West), hypertrophic/restrictive cardiomyopathy.

Diagnosis

NT-proBNP/BNP has excellent negative predictive value. Echocardiography essential (assesses EF, valvular function, diastolic function). ECG, chest X-ray. Cardiac MRI for tissue characterisation if infiltrative disease suspected.

HFrEF Four Pillars of Therapy

All four initiated and up-titrated in parallel per contemporary guidelines:

  • ACE-I/ARB or ARNI: Sacubitril-valsartan superior to ACE-I (PARADIGM-HF)
  • Beta-blocker: Bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol succinate only
  • MRA: Spironolactone or eplerenone (monitor potassium/renal function)
  • SGLT2 inhibitor: Dapagliflozin, empagliflozin — benefit regardless of diabetes status

Device therapy: ICD for primary prevention (EF≤35%), CRT for dyssynchrony.

Rheumatic Heart Disease

Significant cause of heart failure in India, following inadequately treated streptococcal pharyngitis leading to rheumatic fever and valvular damage (mitral valve most commonly affected).

Prevention

Primary: prompt antibiotic treatment of streptococcal sore throat. Secondary: long-term penicillin prophylaxis (benzathine penicillin IM every 3-4 weeks) for confirmed rheumatic fever/RHD.

25-30% of Indian Adults

Hypertension

Research on classification, India burden, and management from ACC/AHA, ESC and IHCI guidelines.

Classification

Per ACC/AHA: Normal <120/80, Elevated 120-129/<80, Stage 1 130-139/80-89, Stage 2 ≥140/90. Hypertensive emergency: severe BP elevation WITH acute target organ damage — requires immediate hospital management.

India Burden

Prevalence 25-30% of adults, with awareness/treatment/control rates significantly lower than high-income countries. High dietary salt intake (2-3x WHO limit) a major contributor. India Hypertension Control Initiative (IHCI) works to improve detection and control through standardized primary care protocols.

Management

Lifestyle: sodium restriction under 5-6g/day, DASH diet, exercise. Pharmacological: ACE-I/ARB, calcium channel blocker, thiazide-like diuretic. Resistant hypertension: add spironolactone (PATHWAY-2), assess adherence, consider secondary causes.

Rhythm Disorders

Cardiac Arrhythmias

Research on atrial fibrillation, SVT, ventricular arrhythmias, and bradyarrhythmias from ESC and ACC/AHA guidelines.

Atrial Fibrillation

Most common sustained arrhythmia. Major concern: 5x increased stroke risk from left atrial appendage thrombus.

Stroke Risk (CHA2DS2-VASc)

Congestive heart failure, Hypertension, Age≥75(2), Diabetes, prior Stroke/TIA(2), Vascular disease, Age 65-74, Sex category female.

Management

DOACs preferred over warfarin for most patients. Rate control (beta-blockers/verapamil/diltiazem) first-line. Catheter ablation increasingly first-line for symptomatic paroxysmal AF.

Supraventricular Tachycardia

Regular narrow-complex tachycardia, often AVNRT or AVRT. Acute: vagal manoeuvres first-line, IV adenosine if fails. Long-term: catheter ablation curative for recurrent symptomatic SVT.

Ventricular Arrhythmias

VT: life-threatening if unstable, immediate DC cardioversion. VF: CARDIAC ARREST, immediate CPR and defibrillation. ICD for secondary prevention after survived arrest or sustained VT.

Bradyarrhythmias

Sinus node dysfunction, AV block (first/second/third degree). Complete heart block requires pacemaker unless reversible cause. Mobitz II and complete heart block are indications for permanent pacing.

Structural Heart Disease

Valvular Heart Disease

Research on aortic stenosis, mitral disease, and endocarditis from ACC/AHA and ESC guidelines.

Aortic Stenosis

Classic triad (poor prognosis if present): exertional angina, syncope, breathlessness. Severe AS: valve area <1.0cm². Management: TAVI increasingly used across risk spectrum, surgical AVR for suitable candidates.

Mitral Disease

Mitral stenosis predominantly rheumatic (relevant in India). Percutaneous balloon mitral valvotomy treatment of choice for suitable anatomy. Mitral regurgitation: surgical repair preferred over replacement where feasible.

Infective Endocarditis

Diagnosis: modified Duke criteria. Management: prolonged IV antibiotics (4-6 weeks), surgery for heart failure/uncontrolled infection/large vegetations. Antibiotic prophylaxis for dental procedures now only for highest-risk patients.

Cardiac Emergencies

🚨
CARDIAC EMERGENCIES — 112 (India) / 999 (UK) / 911 (US): STEMI, cardiac arrest, aortic dissection, massive PE, acute pulmonary oedema. ALL LIFE-THREATENING.

🚨 STEMI / Heart Attack

Central crushing chest pain/pressure, radiating to arm/jaw/neck, sweating, nausea, breathlessness. Atypical in women/elderly/diabetics.

  • Call 112/999/911 IMMEDIATELY
  • Chew aspirin 300mg if available and no contraindication
  • Do NOT drive yourself to hospital
  • Time is muscle — every minute of delay increases damage

🚨 Cardiac Arrest

No pulse, unresponsive, not breathing normally (agonal gasping is NOT normal breathing).

  • Call for emergency help immediately
  • Start chest compressions immediately (100-120/min, depth 5-6cm)
  • Use AED as soon as available
  • Continue CPR until help arrives or patient responds

🚨 Aortic Dissection

Sudden severe tearing/ripping chest or back pain, may migrate. Unequal pulses/BP between arms.

  • EMERGENCY — high mortality if untreated
  • Type A (ascending): surgical emergency
  • CT aortography crucial — thrombolysis would be catastrophic if dissection present

🚨 Acute Pulmonary Oedema

Severe breathlessness worse lying flat, frothy pink sputum, extreme distress.

  • Sit patient upright, high-flow oxygen
  • IV diuretics, nitrates if BP adequate
  • Identify and treat precipitant (ACS, arrhythmia, uncontrolled hypertension)
Cardiovascular Prevention

Prevention & Lifestyle

Research on risk assessment, lipid management, and lifestyle measures from ACC/AHA and ESC guidelines.

Risk Assessment

CV risk calculators (ASCVD, QRISK3, SCORE2) underestimate risk in South Asian populations. Several guidelines recommend lower treatment thresholds for South Asian ethnicity given demonstrated excess cardiovascular risk.

Lipid Management

Statins first-line for primary/secondary prevention. Very high-risk patients target LDL <55mg/dL. PCSK9 inhibitors for very high-risk not at target. Lp(a) increasingly recognised as important independent risk factor, elevated levels common in South Asians.

Lifestyle Measures

150+ minutes/week moderate exercise, Mediterranean/DASH diet, smoking cessation (single most impactful intervention), weight management targeting visceral adiposity. Cardiac rehabilitation post-MI demonstrably improves outcomes and is underutilised.

India Context

Cardiology in India

India-specific cardiovascular burden, treatment access, and CSI guidance.

Burden and Epidemiology

Leading cause of death in India, over a quarter of all deaths. CAD events occur 5-10 years earlier than Western countries. Contributing factors: high diabetes prevalence, abdominal obesity at lower BMI, tobacco use, elevated Lp(a), high salt/refined carbohydrate intake.

Treatment Access

Significant improvement in tertiary cardiac care (AIIMS, private hospitals) offering PCI, CABG, TAVI comparable to international standards. Rural-urban disparity persists for timely primary PCI. Ayushman Bharat (PM-JAY) provides insurance coverage for eligible families. Generic cardiac medications widely available at low cost.

Rheumatic Disease Burden

RHD remains significant in India unlike high-income countries where it is now rare. Community-based echocardiographic screening programmes in schools aim to detect subclinical RHD earlier.
