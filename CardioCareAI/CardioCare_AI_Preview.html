Cardiac Conditions

Deep-researched clinical information on major cardiovascular diseases from ACC/AHA, ESC and CSI guidelines.

⚠️
All information is for educational research. Diagnosis requires clinical evaluation by a qualified cardiologist. Do not self-diagnose cardiac conditions.

Quick Reference — Major Conditions

Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

  • Leading cause of death worldwide. Atherosclerotic plaques narrow coronary arteries reducing blood flow.
  • Spectrum: Stable angina → Unstable angina → NSTEMI → STEMI
  • Risk factors: Hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidaemia, smoking, obesity, family history, age, male sex
  • Investigation: ECG, troponin, stress test, CTCA, coronary angiography (gold standard)
  • Treatment: Lifestyle + ASA + statin + beta-blocker + ACE/ARB + revascularisation (PCI/CABG) when indicated

Heart Failure (HF)

  • HFrEF (EF <40%): Guideline-directed therapy: ACE/ARB/ARNI + beta-blocker + MRA + SGLT2i (now 4 pillars)
  • HFpEF (EF ≥50%): SGLT2i (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) — first approved evidence-based therapy
  • NYHA Classification: I (no symptoms) → II (mild) → III (moderate) → IV (symptoms at rest)
  • Key biomarkers: BNP >35 pg/mL or NT-proBNP >125 pg/mL
  • Device therapy: ICD (if EF <35%), CRT (EF <35% + LBBB >150ms), LVAD as bridge/destination

Atrial Fibrillation (AF)

  • Most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia. 33 million affected globally.
  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc score determines anticoagulation need (≥2 in males, ≥3 in females — anticoagulate)
  • Rate control: Beta-blockers, digoxin, non-DHP CCBs (target resting HR <110 bpm)
  • Rhythm control: Flecainide, propafenone, amiodarone, sotalol, dronedarone, catheter ablation
  • Anticoagulation: DOACs preferred (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) over warfarin

Hypertension

  • Defined: ≥140/90 mmHg (clinic); ≥130/80 (ACC/AHA 2017 definition)
  • Hypertensive Emergency: SBP ≥180 with end-organ damage — immediate IV treatment needed
  • Target: <130/80 in most patients with established CVD or high risk (AHA 2017)
  • First-line: ACE inhibitor/ARB + CCB + thiazide diuretic (single pill combinations preferred)
  • India: Nearly 30% of adults affected; salt intake average 10-12g/day (target <5g/day WHO)

Statins — HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitors
Lipid-Lowering · First-Line for CVD Prevention
Statins are the cornerstone of cardiovascular prevention. They inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, reducing hepatic cholesterol synthesis and upregulating LDL receptors. Benefits extend beyond lipid lowering — anti-inflammatory, plaque-stabilising, antithrombotic effects ("pleiotropic effects"). Key drugs: Rosuvastatin (most potent, 40-80% LDL reduction at 20-40mg) — preferred in South Asians due to high potency at lower dose. Atorvastatin (40-80mg, 40-50% LDL reduction) — most prescribed worldwide. Pravastatin, Simvastatin — moderate intensity. Targets: LDL <70 mg/dL for very high risk (established CVD); LDL <55 mg/dL for extreme risk (recurrent ACS). Key side effects: Myopathy/myalgia (3-5%), rhabdomyolysis (rare <0.1%), hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs at baseline). Statin intolerance: Try alternate-day dosing, lower-intensity statin, rosuvastatin 5mg, or non-statin alternatives (ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitors). Ezetimibe 10mg: Adds 15-20% LDL reduction — first add-on when statin alone insufficient. PCSK9 inhibitors (alirocumab, evolocumab): 60% additional LDL reduction. Reserved for familial hypercholesterolaemia or very high risk not at target on statin + ezetimibe. Inclisiran: siRNA PCSK9 inhibitor — 2 doses/year by injection, 50% LDL reduction.

Lipid Targets (ACC/AHA 2022, ESC 2021)
Risk-Based LDL Goals
Very High Risk (established ASCVD, diabetes with organ damage, CKD 3B-5, FH): LDL <55 mg/dL (<1.4 mmol/L) and ≥50% reduction from baseline. High Risk (DM, CKD 3-4, ≥3 risk factors): LDL <70 mg/dL (<1.8 mmol/L). Moderate Risk: LDL <100 mg/dL (<2.6 mmol/L). Non-HDL cholesterol targets are 30 mg/dL higher than LDL targets. Triglycerides >500 mg/dL: Treat with fibrates (fenofibrate, gemfibrozil) or omega-3 (icosapent ethyl — Vascepa for CV risk reduction).
💊
Beta-Blockers (β-Adrenergic Blockers)
Sympatholytic · Cardiac Rate & Contractility Reduction
Beta-blockers block catecholamine (adrenaline) effects on β₁-receptors (heart) and β₂-receptors (lungs, peripheral). Essential therapy for multiple cardiac conditions. Carvedilol (non-selective + α₁ blocker): Preferred in HFrEF. Start 3.125mg BD, uptitrate to 25-50mg BD. Metoprolol succinate (β₁-selective, extended release): HFrEF, post-MI, hypertension, rate control in AF. 25-200mg OD. Bisoprolol (highly β₁-selective): HFrEF, rate control. 1.25-10mg OD. Atenolol (β₁-selective): Hypertension, angina — less preferred for HF. Nebivolol: Highly selective, NO-mediated vasodilation — useful in elderly HFpEF. Indications: HFrEF (mortality benefit), Post-MI (reduces reinfarction, mortality), AF rate control, Angina, Hypertension, HCM (reduces LVOTO gradient), Aortic dissection (IV esmolol/labetalol). Absolute contraindications: Cardiogenic shock, decompensated HF (acute), high-degree AV block (without pacemaker), severe asthma/COPD (relative). Key side effects: Bradycardia, hypotension, fatigue, erectile dysfunction, masks hypoglycaemia symptoms in diabetics, cold extremities, worsening peripheral vascular disease.

ACE Inhibitors / ARBs / ARNI
RAAS Blockade · Cornerstone of HF & Post-MI Therapy
ACE Inhibitors: Block conversion of Ang I → Ang II. Reduce afterload, prevent cardiac remodelling. Ramipril, Enalapril, Lisinopril, Perindopril, Captopril. Essential in HFrEF, post-MI LV dysfunction, hypertension, diabetic nephropathy. Target doses: Ramipril 10mg OD, Enalapril 20mg BD, Lisinopril 35mg OD. Key side effects: Dry cough (10-15% — angiotensin II-mediated — switch to ARB), Hyperkalaemia, Renal impairment (monitor in first 2 weeks), Angioedema (rare but life-threatening — discontinue immediately). Contraindicated: Bilateral renal artery stenosis, pregnancy, angioedema history. ARBs (Angiotensin Receptor Blockers): Block AT₁ receptor directly. No cough. Losartan, Valsartan, Candesartan, Irbesartan, Olmesartan, Telmisartan. Use when ACE inhibitor not tolerated. ARNI (Angiotensin Receptor-Neprilysin Inhibitor): Sacubitril/Valsartan (Entresto) — Superior to enalapril in HFrEF (PARADIGM-HF trial: 20% mortality reduction). First-line in ambulatory HFrEF patients. Do not give within 36 hours of ACE inhibitor (risk of angioedema). MRA (Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonist): Spironolactone, Eplerenone — 4th pillar of HFrEF treatment. 25-50mg OD. Monitor potassium. SGLT2 Inhibitors: Dapagliflozin (Farxiga), Empagliflozin (Jardiance) — now the 5th pillar. Proven HF hospitalisation and CV death reduction in HFrEF AND HFpEF.

Anticoagulants
Thrombosis Prevention · Essential for AF, VTE, Mechanical Valves
DOACs (Direct Oral Anticoagulants) — Preferred in non-valvular AF and VTE: Apixaban (Eliquis) 5mg BD: AF, VTE. Preferred in elderly, renal impairment. ARISTOTLE trial: Superior to warfarin (less bleeding, less stroke, less mortality). Rivaroxaban (Xarelto) 20mg OD with evening meal: AF, VTE, post-ACS (2.5mg BD with ASA). Dabigatran (Pradaxa): 150mg BD (AF), 110mg BD (elderly >80 or high bleeding risk). Direct thrombin inhibitor. Avoid if CrCl <30. Reversal: Idarucizumab (Praxbind). Edoxaban 60mg OD: AF and VTE. Reversal agents: Andexanet alfa — reverses apixaban and rivaroxaban. Idarucizumab — reverses dabigatran. 4-PCC (prothrombin complex concentrate) — emergency reversal. Warfarin: Still required for mechanical heart valves and AF with mitral stenosis. INR target: 2-3 (AF, VTE), 2.5-3.5 (mechanical mitral valve). Frequent monitoring. Multiple drug and food interactions (Vitamin K-containing foods). Heparins: UFH (IV) and LMWH (enoxaparin SC) — acute ACS, VTE treatment, bridging. Fondaparinux — preferred in HIT risk. HIT (Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia): Immune-mediated platelet drop + thrombosis. Stop all heparin. Use argatroban or danaparoid.

Antiplatelet Agents
Platelet Inhibition · Essential in ACS and Post-PCI
Aspirin (ASA): Irreversibly inhibits COX-1, reducing thromboxane A₂-mediated platelet aggregation. 75-100mg OD for secondary prevention. Loading dose 300mg in ACS. Contraindicated: Active peptic ulcer (use PPI cover), aspirin allergy, severe bleeding risk. P2Y12 Inhibitors: Clopidogrel 75mg OD (300-600mg loading): Pro-drug, requires CYP2C19 activation. 10-15% are poor metabolisers (genetic testing: CYP2C19*2/*3). Duration with ASA (DAPT): 12 months post-ACS, 1-6 months post-elective PCI. Ticagrelor (Brilinta) 90mg BD: Direct P2Y12 blocker. Faster, stronger, more consistent than clopidogrel. PLATO trial: Superior to clopidogrel in ACS. Side effects: Dyspnoea (14%), bradycardia. Loading: 180mg. Prasugrel (Efient) 10mg OD: Most potent P2Y12 blocker. TRITON-TIMI: Superior in STEMI undergoing PCI. Avoid: Age ≥75, weight <60kg, prior stroke/TIA (net harm). Loading: 60mg. DAPT (Dual Antiplatelet Therapy): ASA + P2Y12 inhibitor. Standard post-ACS and post-PCI. Duration: Minimum 12 months post-ACS, 1-6 months post-stable PCI (shorter if high bleeding risk). DAPT score/PRECISE-DAPT guides duration. Cangrelor: IV P2Y12 inhibitor — for patients unable to take oral medication during PCI. Reversal within 60 minutes of stopping.

Diuretics
Fluid Management · Heart Failure & Hypertension
Loop Diuretics (most potent): Furosemide (Lasix) 20-400mg: Acts on thick ascending loop of Henle. Decongestion in acute HF. IV in acute setting (0.5-1mg/kg), oral in chronic. Risk of hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia, ototoxicity (high IV doses), dehydration. Torasemide 5-20mg: Longer acting, better oral bioavailability than furosemide. Preferred for chronic HF maintenance. Bumetanide. Thiazide Diuretics: Hydrochlorothiazide 12.5-25mg, Indapamide 1.25-2.5mg — First-line for hypertension. Synergistic with loop diuretics in resistant oedema. Side effects: Hypokalaemia, hyperuricaemia, hyperglycaemia, hyponatraemia. Avoid in gout. Potassium-Sparing Diuretics: Spironolactone (MRA) 25-50mg: HFrEF mortality benefit (RALES trial), resistant hypertension (4th-line), hyperaldosteronism. Monitor K⁺, renal function. Eplerenone 25-50mg: More selective MRA, fewer hormonal side effects. Post-MI HFrEF (EPHESUS trial). Amiloride 5-10mg: Used with thiazides to prevent hypokalaemia. SGLT2 Inhibitors (Osmotic Diuretics): Dapagliflozin 10mg, Empagliflozin 10mg — new class with diuretic properties. Unlike traditional diuretics, do not activate RAAS. HFrEF and HFpEF benefits irrespective of diabetes.

Antiarrhythmic Drugs (Vaughan Williams Classification)
Rhythm Control · Rate Control
Class Ia (Na⁺ channel blockers, intermediate): Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide. Largely historical — proarrhythmic risk. Disopyramide still used in HCM with LVOTO. Class Ib: Lidocaine (IV) — Acute VT during/post-MI. Mexiletine (oral) — VT suppression. Class Ic (most potent Na⁺ blockers): Flecainide 50-150mg BD, Propafenone — AF rhythm control in structurally normal hearts. "Pill-in-pocket" approach for paroxysmal AF. CONTRAINDICATED in CAD, HF, LV dysfunction (CAST trial: increased mortality). Class II (Beta-blockers): See Beta-Blockers section. Rate control, VT suppression. Class III (K⁺ channel blockers): Amiodarone — Most effective antiarrhythmic. AF, VT, VF. Loading 200mg TDS → 200mg OD maintenance. Multiple toxicities (thyroid — hypo/hyper, pulmonary fibrosis, hepatotoxicity, corneal deposits, photosensitivity, peripheral neuropathy, bradycardia). Monitor TFTs, PFTs, LFTs, ophthal annually. Sotalol 40-160mg BD: Beta-blocking + K⁺ blocking. AF, VT maintenance. Prolongs QT — risk of Torsades de Pointes. Renal dose adjustment essential. Dronedarone 400mg BD: Multi-channel blocker, less toxic than amiodarone. AF rhythm control. CONTRAINDICATED in HFrEF. Ibutilide, Dofetilide (Class III IV/oral): Chemical cardioversion of AF. Class IV (Ca²⁺ channel blockers): Verapamil 40-120mg TDS, Diltiazem — Rate control in AF, SVT termination (verapamil IV). Do NOT combine with beta-blocker IV.

Other Essential Cardiac Drugs
Nitrates · CCBs · Ivabradine · Digoxin · Vasopressors
Nitrates: GTN (Glyceryl trinitrate) spray/sublingual — Acute angina relief. IV GTN — Acute HF, unstable angina. Isosorbide mononitrate/dinitrate (oral, long-acting) — Chronic angina. Tolerance develops with continuous use — nitrate-free interval of 8-10 hours essential. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs): Amlodipine 2.5-10mg — DHP CCB. Hypertension, angina (vasospastic and stable), peripheral oedema (common side effect). Does not suppress AV node. Lercanidipine — Fewer peripheral oedema side effects than amlodipine. Nifedipine (long-acting only) — Hypertension, angina, Raynaud's. AVOID short-acting sublingual nifedipine (precipitous BP drop). Ivabradine (Coralan) 5-7.5mg BD: Pure heart rate reduction via If channel inhibition in SA node. HFrEF with HR ≥70 on maximum beta-blocker (SHIFT trial: reduced HF hospitalisation). Stable angina with intolerance to beta-blockers. Avoid: AF. Digoxin: Positive inotrope, AV node blocker. Rate control in AF when beta-blockers/CCBs insufficient. HFrEF symptom control (does not reduce mortality). Narrow therapeutic index — toxicity: nausea, visual changes (xanthopsia — yellow-green vision), arrhythmias. Target level: 0.5-0.9 ng/mL. Toxicity enhanced by hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia. Vasopressors/Inotropes (ICU): Dobutamine, Noradrenaline, Dopamine — Cardiogenic shock. Milrinone (PDE3 inhibitor) — Bridge to transplant/LVAD. Levosimendan — Calcium sensitiser, positive inodilatation.

Cardiac Procedures & Surgery

Comprehensive research on interventional cardiology procedures, cardiac surgery, and device therapies from ACC/AHA and ESC guidelines.

Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI / Angioplasty)

PCI uses a catheter with a balloon (and usually a stent) inserted via the femoral or radial artery to open blocked coronary arteries. Radial access now preferred (less bleeding — RIVAL, MATRIX trials). STEMI PCI: Primary PCI within 90 minutes of first medical contact is the gold standard for STEMI (door-to-balloon <90 min). Superior to thrombolysis when achievable in time. Stent types: Bare Metal Stents (BMS) — largely obsolete. Drug-Eluting Stents (DES) — current standard. Release sirolimus/everolimus/zotarolimus to prevent restenosis. 2nd-generation DES have excellent safety profile. Bioresorbable Vascular Scaffolds (BVS) — biodegradable; limited role currently. Post-PCI care: DAPT (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor) for minimum 12 months post-ACS, 1-6 months post-stable PCI. Annual follow-up ECG and stress test.

Key Trials Supporting PCI

  • GUSTO, PAMI — Established primary PCI superiority over thrombolysis in STEMI
  • COURAGE — PCI + optimal medical therapy vs optimal medical therapy alone in stable CAD: No mortality difference. PCI provides better angina relief.
  • ISCHEMIA — Invasive vs conservative strategy in stable CAD with moderate-severe ischaemia: No mortality difference at 3-5 years
  • SYNTAX — Compared PCI vs CABG in 3-vessel disease: CABG superior for complex disease (SYNTAX score >22)

Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG)

Surgery that uses arterial/venous grafts to bypass blocked coronary arteries. On-pump (cardiopulmonary bypass) or off-pump (beating heart CABG). Graft types: Left Internal Mammary Artery (LIMA) to LAD — gold standard, 95%+ patency at 10 years. Radial artery — excellent patency. Right IMA (RIMA) — bilateral IMA (reduces sternal wound infection risk). Saphenous vein graft (SVG) — 50% occluded at 10 years (inferior to arterial grafts).

Indications (ACC/AHA 2021)

  • Left main coronary artery stenosis ≥50% (Class I indication)
  • 3-vessel CAD with EF <35% — CABG superior to PCI (STICH trial)
  • 3-vessel CAD with diabetes — CABG superior (FREEDOM trial)
  • Complex 3-vessel CAD (high SYNTAX score >22)
  • Failed/unsuitable PCI with ongoing ischaemia
  • Concomitant cardiac surgery (valve repair/replacement + revascularisation)

TAVR/TAVI — Transcatheter Aortic Valve Replacement

Minimally invasive valve replacement via transfemoral (TF) or transapical approach without open-heart surgery. Now indicated for all surgical risk groups based on PARTNER 3 and Evolut Low Risk trials (low risk, intermediate risk, high risk, inoperable). Valve types: Edwards SAPIEN (balloon-expandable), Medtronic Evolut PRO (self-expanding). Ongoing debate on valve durability vs surgical valves (15-20 year data not yet available for TAVR). Post-TAVR: Aspirin 75mg + clopidogrel 75mg for 3-6 months, then aspirin alone. If anticoagulation needed: DOAC + aspirin (no additional P2Y12).

Other Transcatheter Procedures

  • MitraClip / TEER: Transcatheter edge-to-edge repair for mitral regurgitation — COAPT trial: reduced HF hospitalisation and mortality in secondary MR
  • TMVR (Transcatheter Mitral Valve Replacement): Emerging — for severe mitral disease in high-surgical-risk patients
  • TPVR (Tricuspid): TriClip, CLASP — Emerging for severe TR
  • Balloon Mitral Valvotomy (BMV/PTMC): For rheumatic mitral stenosis with favourable valve morphology — still widely used in India/developing world

Electrophysiology (EP) Procedures

Catheter Ablation

  • AF Ablation (Pulmonary Vein Isolation — PVI): RF or cryoablation isolates pulmonary veins (triggers of AF). 70-80% freedom from AF at 1 year for paroxysmal AF. Less effective for persistent AF. CABANA and EAST-AF trials: Better QoL and rhythm control vs drugs.
  • SVT Ablation (AVNRT, WPW): Curative in 95%+ of cases. First-line for symptomatic SVT. WPW — ablate accessory pathway.
  • VT Ablation: For structural heart disease VT. Reduces VT episodes and ICD shocks. Not curative for ischaemic VT (70% success).
  • AFL (Typical Atrial Flutter) Ablation: Cavotricuspid isthmus ablation — curative in 95%.
  • Pulsed Field Ablation (PFA): Newest technology — electroporation-based, tissue-selective (spares oesophagus, phrenic nerve), faster procedure times. Now replacing RF/cryo in many centres.

Pacemakers (PM)

  • Single chamber (VVI/AAI): Rate-responsive PM. For simple SAN/AV block in AF or normal sinus node
  • Dual chamber (DDD): Senses and paces both chambers. Physiological pacing. For complete heart block with normal sinus function
  • CRT (Cardiac Resynchronisation Therapy): BiV pacing. HFrEF (EF ≤35%) + LBBB (QRS ≥150ms) + NYHA II-IV. 30% reduction in mortality and hospitalisation
  • Leadless PM (Micra): Implanted directly in RV. No leads, no pocket. For patients at high infection risk or poor venous access
  • His-bundle/LBBP pacing: Physiological pacing — emerging alternative to CRT for pacing-induced cardiomyopathy

ICD — Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator

  • Primary prevention: HFrEF with EF ≤35% on optimal medical therapy ≥3 months (MADIT II, SCD-HeFT trials)
  • Secondary prevention: Survivors of VF/VT arrest without reversible cause
  • S-ICD (Subcutaneous ICD): No intracardiac leads. No pacing capability. For patients at lead infection risk. UNTOUCHED trial: comparable to transvenous ICD
  • Wearable ICD (LifeVest): External defibrillator vest. Bridge use: post-MI (90 days), post-revascularisation, newly diagnosed cardiomyopathy (3-6 months)

ECG — Systematic Interpretation Approach

The ECG records electrical activity. A systematic approach: Rate → Rhythm → Axis → P waves → PR interval → QRS → ST segment → T waves → QT interval.

Normal Values

ParameterNormal RangeSignificance of Abnormality
Heart Rate60-100 bpm<60 bradycardia; >100 tachycardia; >150 sustained = serious arrhythmia
PR Interval120-200 ms (3-5 small squares)>200ms = 1st degree AV block; absent P = AF/junctional; short = WPW/LGL
QRS Duration<120 ms (<3 small squares)>120ms = bundle branch block or ventricular rhythm; >160ms = VT concern
QTc Interval<440ms (men), <460ms (women)>500ms = high Torsades de Pointes risk — check drugs, electrolytes
ST SegmentIsoelectric ±0.5mmElevation ≥1mm (2mm V2-V3): STEMI until proven otherwise. Depression: ischaemia, posterior MI
T WaveUpright (except aVR, V1)Inversion: ischaemia, LVH, RBBB, PE (V1-V4), Wellens (V2-V3)
Axis-30° to +90°LAD <-30°: LAFB, inferior MI, HCM. RAD >90°: LPFB, RVH, PE, lateral MI

Key ECG Patterns to Recognise

  • STEMI territories: Inferior (II,III,aVF — RCA), Anterior (V1-V4 — LAD), Lateral (I,aVL,V5-V6 — LCx), Posterior (R>S in V1, ST depression V1-V3 — mirror image)
  • LBBB: QRS ≥120ms, broad notched R in V5/V6/I, rS in V1. Sgarbossa criteria for STEMI in LBBB. New LBBB in ACS context = treat as STEMI
  • RBBB: RSR' ("M pattern") in V1, broad slurred S in I/V6. Right ventricular strain pattern: S1Q3T3 in PE
  • WPW (Wolff-Parkinson-White): Short PR <120ms, delta wave, wide QRS. Risk of rapid AF conducting via accessory pathway. AVOID AV nodal drugs (adenosine, verapamil, digoxin) — can precipitate VF
  • Brugada pattern: Coved ST elevation ≥2mm in V1-V2 with RBBB morphology. Risk of sudden cardiac death. ICD indicated for symptomatic patients
  • Long QT: Congenital (LQTS1-3) or acquired (drugs: sotalol, amiodarone, antipsychotics, antibiotics; electrolytes: ↓K, ↓Mg, ↓Ca)
  • Hyperkalaemia ECG: Peaked T waves → PR prolongation → P wave flattening → Sine wave → VF. Treat urgently (calcium gluconate IV)
  • Digoxin effect: "Reverse tick" ST depression, short QT, bidirectional VT in toxicity

Risk Enhancers — Factors That Increase ASCVD Risk Beyond PCE

  • Family History of premature ASCVD (<55 male, <65 female first-degree relative) — one of the most powerful individual risk factors
  • High-sensitivity CRP ≥2.0 mg/L — independent predictor (JUPITER trial)
  • Lipoprotein(a) ≥125 nmol/L (50 mg/dL) — genetically elevated, adds 2-3x risk
  • ABI (Ankle-Brachial Index) <0.9 — indicates peripheral arterial disease
  • Coronary Artery Calcium (CAC) Score: Most powerful reclassifier. CAC=0: very low risk (statin may be withheld); CAC ≥100 or ≥75th percentile: high risk (start statin regardless of risk score)
  • South Asian ethnicity — 1.5-2x higher CAD risk than European ancestry at same traditional risk factor level. Diabetes more prevalent, earlier onset. Metabolic syndrome common even at normal BMI
  • Metabolic syndrome (3 of 5: central obesity, TG ≥150, low HDL, BP ≥130/85, glucose ≥100)
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) — Independent CV risk factor, especially eGFR <45
  • Autoimmune/inflammatory conditions — Rheumatoid arthritis (1.5-2x CV risk), SLE, psoriasis
  • Preterm birth, pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes in women — long-term CV risk markers
  • HIV/AIDS on ART — Accelerated atherosclerosis
Cardiac Nutrition

Cardiac Diet & Nutrition

Evidence-based cardiac dietary protocols from ACC/AHA, AHA Dietary Guidelines, and landmark nutrition trials.

AHA Heart-Healthy Dietary Principles (2021)

Emphasise

  • Vegetables and fruits — 4-5 cups/day. Wide variety. Berries (anthocyanins), leafy greens (nitrates, folate), tomatoes (lycopene), cruciferous (sulforaphane)
  • Whole grains — At least half of grain intake. Oats (beta-glucan lowers LDL 5-10%), barley, brown rice, whole wheat. Soluble fibre target: 10-25g/day for LDL reduction
  • Healthy protein sources — Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring) 2+ portions/week. Legumes (beans, lentils, chickpeas, dal). Plant proteins. Lean poultry (without skin)
  • Nuts and seeds — Walnuts (ALA omega-3), almonds (Vit E, monounsaturated), flaxseeds (ALA, lignans), chia seeds. 30g/day
  • Healthy oils — Extra-virgin olive oil (EVOO) — oleocanthal, polyphenols; proven CV benefit in PREDIMED. Canola oil. Avoid partial hydrogenation
  • Omega-3 fatty acids — Marine EPA + DHA (oily fish, fish oil). AHA: 1g EPA+DHA/day for CAD. REDUCE-IT trial: Icosapent ethyl (Vascepa) 4g/day — 25% reduction in CV events in hypertriglyceridaemia on statin
  • Potassium-rich foods — Counters sodium, lowers BP. Bananas, potatoes (with skin), avocado, beans, spinach, tomatoes. Target: 4700mg/day

Reduce / Avoid

  • Sodium — Target <2300mg/day (AHA); <1500mg/day for hypertension. 1 teaspoon salt = 2300mg. Each 1g sodium reduction lowers BP ~1-2 mmHg. Hidden sodium: processed foods (80% of intake), bread, canned foods, sauces, pickles
  • Saturated fat — <6% of total calories (AHA). Replaces saturated with polyunsaturated fat reduces CVD events (PREDIMED, STARS). Sources: butter, ghee, full-fat dairy, fatty red meat, coconut oil, palm oil
  • Trans fats — Eliminate completely. Artificial trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils) — worst dietary predictor of CVD. Now banned in many countries. Watch: packaged fried foods, commercial baked goods
  • Added sugars — <10% of calories (AHA: <25g/day women, <36g men). Sugar-sweetened beverages — most harmful. Fructose overload → hypertriglyceridaemia, hepatic steatosis
  • Alcohol — No beneficial CV effect with low-to-moderate consumption (Mendelian randomisation studies). AHA 2023: No safe amount. If drinking: <1 standard drink/day women, <2 men. Increases: AF, hypertension, cardiomyopathy, hypertriglyceridaemia
  • Red and processed meat — Processed meat (bacon, sausage, deli meat): 50g/day = 18% higher CAD risk (EPIC). Red meat: 100g/day = 15% higher risk. TMAO (trimethylamine N-oxide) — gut microbiome-derived metabolite from carnitine/choline in meat — linked to CV events
  • Ultra-processed foods — NutriScore rating. Each 10% increase in UPF consumption = 12% higher CV mortality (BMJ 2019 cohort)

Mediterranean Diet — PREDIMED Trial Evidence

PREDIMED trial (New England Journal of Medicine 2013): Mediterranean diet + extra-virgin olive oil or nuts reduced major CV events by 30% compared to low-fat diet in high-risk individuals. One of the most powerful dietary interventions for CV prevention.

Mediterranean Diet Principles

  • Olive oil (EVOO) — Primary fat source. 4+ tablespoons/day in PREDIMED. Rich in oleocanthal (anti-inflammatory, COX inhibitor similar to ibuprofen), oleic acid, polyphenols
  • Vegetables — 2+ portions at lunch and dinner. Mediterranean staples: tomatoes, aubergine, courgette, artichokes, peppers
  • Legumes — 3+ times/week. Chickpeas, lentils, beans — protein, fibre, low GI
  • Fruit — 3+ portions/day. Including tree nuts (walnuts 30g/day in PREDIMED nuts arm)
  • Fish/seafood — 3+ times/week. Oily fish provides EPA+DHA
  • Whole grains — Preferred over refined grains
  • Poultry — Preferred over red meat. Red meat <1/week
  • Red wine — Moderate (1 glass/day with meals, optional) — resveratrol, polyphenols. NOTE: AHA 2023 recommends no alcohol
  • Avoid: Butter/margarine (<1 tsp/day), commercial bakery/pastry (<3/week), sweetened drinks, processed meat (<1/week), full-fat dairy

Indian Adaptation of Mediterranean Diet

  • Replace olive oil with mustard oil (high ALA omega-3 and erucic acid — controversial; WHO-approved for cooking) or canola oil
  • Dal/rajma/chana = Mediterranean legumes
  • Turmeric, curcumin — anti-inflammatory, CV benefits in trials (though bioavailability remains debate)
  • Fenugreek (methi) — soluble fibre, LDL reduction
  • Amla (Indian gooseberry) — Vitamin C, antioxidant, CV benefits in small trials
  • Walnuts (akhrot) 30g/day, almonds (badam) 30g/day — proven LDL reduction

Cholesterol & Lipid-Lowering Foods

Foods That Lower LDL Cholesterol (Specific Evidence)

  • Oats and barley (Beta-glucan): 3g/day beta-glucan soluble fibre → LDL ↓5-10%. 1 bowl oatmeal (40g) provides ~2g beta-glucan. FDA approved heart health claim
  • Plant sterols/stanols: 2g/day → LDL ↓8-10%. Block cholesterol absorption in gut. Found in fortified margarines, yoghurts, juices. Benecol, Flora ProActiv.
  • Soy protein: 25g/day → LDL ↓4-6%. Tofu, soy milk, edamame
  • Psyllium husk (Isabgol): 10-12g/day → LDL ↓7-8%. Widely used in India. Dissolve in water. Also lowers blood glucose and BP
  • Almonds: 30-45g/day → LDL ↓5-7%, increases HDL. Rich in Vit E, monounsaturated FA
  • Walnuts: 30g/day → LDL ↓5-7%, ApoB ↓, Total:HDL ratio ↓. ALA omega-3. WAHA trial
  • Berries: Blueberries, strawberries — Anthocyanins reduce LDL oxidation and inflammation
  • Garlic: Modest LDL reduction (1-5%). Allicin compound. Consistent though small effect across meta-analyses
  • Green tea: 3-5 cups/day — catechins reduce LDL absorption. Meta-analysis: LDL ↓5 mg/dL

Foods That Raise HDL Cholesterol

  • EVOO and olive polyphenols — increase HDL and improve HDL function
  • Fatty fish (EPA+DHA) — raises HDL, reduces TG
  • Avocado — raises HDL, reduces LDL (GRADES study)
  • Purple/red grapes — resveratrol
  • Regular physical exercise — most powerful natural HDL raiser (+10-20%)
  • Weight loss — each 3kg loss raises HDL ~1 mg/dL
  • Stop smoking — immediately raises HDL by ~5%

Foods That Lower Triglycerides

  • Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA+DHA) — most powerful TG reducers. Fish oil 3-4g/day: TG ↓25-40%
  • Reduce refined carbs and added sugars — major drivers of high TG
  • Reduce alcohol — even moderate drinking raises TG significantly
  • Lose weight — each 10kg loss reduces TG ~20%

Heart Failure Diet Protocol

Sodium Restriction in HF

  • <2000mg sodium/day in symptomatic HF (NYHA II-IV) — standard recommendation
  • SODIUM-HF trial (NEJM 2022): Low sodium (<1500mg) vs usual sodium (2300mg) — no significant difference in clinical outcomes. BUT: symptoms, QoL, and KCCQ score improved in low-sodium arm. Individualised approach recommended.
  • Practical: No added salt, avoid canned foods, pickles, papad, processed snacks, sauces

Fluid Restriction in HF

  • 1.5-2 litres/day in advanced HF with hyponatraemia or severe congestion
  • Daily weight monitoring — weigh each morning after voiding, before breakfast
  • Alert cardiologist if weight increases >2kg in 2-3 days (sign of fluid retention)
  • All fluids count: water, tea, coffee, soups, ice cream, fruits with high water content

Nutrition in Advanced HF (Cardiac Cachexia)

  • 30-50% of advanced HF patients develop cardiac cachexia — involuntary weight loss
  • High protein diet: 1.5-2g protein/kg/day to preserve muscle mass
  • Small frequent calorie-dense meals (large meals increase cardiac work)
  • Micronutrient supplementation: Thiamine (furosemide depletes — HF risk), CoQ10 (mitochondrial support), zinc, magnesium

Exercise Protocols for Heart Patients

Evidence-based exercise guidance for patients with various cardiac conditions — from ACC/AHA and ESC exercise in cardiac patients guidelines.

⚠️
Exercise in cardiac patients requires medical clearance. Start exercise programme only after evaluation and approval from your cardiologist. Stop and seek medical attention if chest pain, breathlessness, dizziness, or palpitations occur during exercise.

AHA Exercise Recommendations (General Adults)

  • Moderate intensity aerobic exercise: 150-300 minutes/week OR
  • Vigorous intensity aerobic exercise: 75-150 minutes/week OR
  • Equivalent combination of moderate and vigorous activity
  • Muscle-strengthening activities: ≥2 days/week (all major muscle groups)
  • Reduce sedentary time: Any movement is better than none. Even 10-min bouts of activity are beneficial

Exercise for Specific Cardiac Conditions

  • Post-MI / Post-PCI / Post-CABG: Formal cardiac rehabilitation (see Rehab section). Walking from Day 1 if uncomplicated. Avoid heavy lifting for 6 weeks post-sternotomy. Return to light work: 2-6 weeks. Return to sexual activity: 2-4 weeks (equivalent to climbing 2 flights of stairs).

  • Atrial Fibrillation: Regular exercise reduces AF burden. Oslo Atriale Fibrillasjonsstudie (OAFA) trial: Exercise training reduces AF episodes. Avoid very high-intensity endurance training (marathon, triathlon training) — associated with higher AF risk in athletes.
  • Hypertension: Aerobic exercise lowers SBP by 5-8 mmHg, DBP by 3-4 mmHg (equivalent to one drug). Dynamic resistance training: SBP ↓4 mmHg. Isometric training (wall sit, plank holds): SBP ↓8 mmHg (Wiley meta-analysis 2023 — most effective single exercise modality).
  • HCM (Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy): Historically restricted. RESET-HCM trial (2024): Moderate exercise does not increase arrhythmia risk. Avoid: competitive sports, sudden very vigorous bursts, contact sports. Walking, cycling, swimming at moderate intensity generally permitted.
  • Stable Angina: Exercise training improves ischaemic threshold. Regular walking 30 min/day reduces angina episodes. Warm up and cool down essential (cold exposure triggers coronary vasospasm).
URGENT — Emergency Guide

Cardiac Emergency Guide

🚨
IF YOU OR SOMEONE HAS: Chest pain or tightness, Pain spreading to jaw, arm, or back, Sudden breathlessness, Loss of consciousness, Collapse — CALL 112 (India) / 999 (UK) / 911 (US) IMMEDIATELY. DO NOT DRIVE YOURSELF. Time is muscle — every minute of delay in a heart attack = 2 million heart cells lost.

Recognising a Heart Attack (AMI)

Classic Symptoms

  • Central chest pain / tightness / pressure / squeezing — "elephant sitting on chest"
  • Radiation to left arm, jaw, neck, back, or right arm
  • Sweating (diaphoresis) — cold sweat
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Breathlessness
  • Sense of impending doom ("angor animi")

Atypical Symptoms (More Common in Women, Diabetics, Elderly)

  • Epigastric (stomach) pain or indigestion — can mimic GERD
  • Fatigue and weakness alone (especially in women)
  • Jaw pain or toothache without dental cause
  • Back pain between shoulder blades
  • Lightheadedness or dizziness
  • Palpitations alone (in elderly and diabetics who can have "silent MI")

While Waiting for Ambulance (If Conscious and Not Allergic)

  • Call 112/999/911 FIRST — then follow dispatcher instructions
  • Chew (not swallow) 300mg aspirin (unless allergic or told not to by doctor)
  • Sit or lie in most comfortable position — half-sitting preferred
  • Loosen tight clothing
  • GTN spray (if prescribed): 1 spray under tongue. Repeat after 5 min. Maximum 3 sprays. Do NOT use if Sildenafil/Tadalafil (Viagra/Cialis) taken in last 24-48 hours — severe hypotension
  • Unlock front door for ambulance crew
  • Stay on phone with emergency dispatcher until help arrives

CPR — Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (If Person Collapses and Unresponsive)

  • 1. CHECK — Tap shoulders, shout "Are you OK?" Check breathing (no more than 10 seconds)
  • 2. CALL — 112/999/911. Use speaker phone so hands are free
  • 3. COMPRESS — 30 chest compressions, centre of chest, 5-6cm depth, 100-120/minute ("Stayin' Alive" beat). Arms straight, interlock fingers, heel of hand on chest
  • 4. RESCUE BREATHS (if trained): 2 rescue breaths after 30 compressions. If untrained: Hands-only CPR (compression-only) is effective and now recommended for untrained bystanders
  • 5. AED — If available, use as soon as possible. Turn on, follow voice instructions. AEDs are now in airports, malls, gyms, railway stations
  • Continue 30:2 ratio until: Emergency services arrive, person recovers, AED delivers shock, you are too exhausted

Hypertensive Emergency

  • Definition: Severely elevated BP (usually SBP >180 or DBP >120) WITH evidence of acute end-organ damage
  • End-organ damage: Hypertensive encephalopathy, acute LV failure, aortic dissection, ACS, acute kidney injury, papilloedema, eclampsia
  • Action: Call emergency services. IV antihypertensives: Labetalol, Nicardipine, Sodium nitroprusside, Hydralazine. Target: reduce MAP by 25% in first hour (NOT to normal — cerebral autoregulation must adapt)
  • Aortic dissection: Target HR <60 bpm and SBP 100-120. IV beta-blocker (esmolol) FIRST, then vasodilator. Do NOT give vasodilator alone — increases shear stress
  • Hypertensive urgency (severe BP without end-organ damage): Oral medications, gradual reduction over 24-48 hours. No IV drugs needed. Avoid rapid reduction.
Recovery & Rehabilitation

Cardiac Rehabilitation

Cardiac rehabilitation reduces mortality by 26-31% in post-MI patients (Cochrane review). It is one of the most underutilised yet effective cardiac interventions.

Phases of Cardiac Rehabilitation

Phase 1 — Inpatient (Hospital)

  • Early mobilisation: Sitting up Day 1, walking in corridor Day 2-3
  • Patient and family education: Heart disease understanding, medication compliance, risk factor management
  • Psychological assessment: Depression screening (present in 20-30% post-MI)
  • Risk stratification for Phase 2 exercise intensity
  • Discharge planning: Follow-up appointments, medications, activity restrictions, red flag symptoms

Phase 2 — Outpatient (Weeks 2-12 post-event)

  • Supervised exercise sessions: 2-3x/week for 6-12 weeks, 36 sessions total (AHA)
  • Exercise: Aerobic walking/cycling/swimming + resistance training
  • ECG monitoring during exercise — detect arrhythmias and ischaemia
  • Dietary counselling, smoking cessation, stress management
  • Psychosocial support — CBT for depression and anxiety
  • Medication review and optimisation
  • 30% mortality reduction in those who complete Phase 2 (Cochrane 2016)

Phase 3 — Long-term Maintenance (Ongoing)

  • Continued independent exercise: 150+ min moderate exercise/week
  • Annual review with cardiologist: lipids, BP, weight, HbA1c
  • Ongoing risk factor management
  • Support groups and community exercise

Evidence Base

  • Cochrane review 2016 (63 trials, 14,000 patients): Cardiac rehab reduces all-cause mortality 26%, CV mortality 36%, hospitalisation 18%
  • Despite evidence, uptake is poor: Only 20-30% of eligible patients complete cardiac rehab (UK data), likely lower in India
  • Home-based cardiac rehab (REACH-HF, REMOTE-CR) — non-inferior to centre-based when supervised. Expanded access during COVID.
  • India-specific challenge: Very few structured cardiac rehab programmes. Most major cardiac centres (AIIMS, Medanta, Apollo) now have rehab units
